1. Understanding Reasoning from Pretraining to Post-Training [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Jingyan Shen, Ang Li, Salman Rahman, Yifan Sun, Micah Goldblum, Matus Telgarsky, Pavel Izmailov
   Affiliation: New York University, Modal Labs, UCLA, UIUC, Columbia University
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.16097
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.16097
   Summary: Uses chess as a controlled, fully observable testbed to study how pretraining choices shape
   the returns to RL post-training compute. Pretrains language models from 5M to 1B parameters on human
   chess games, applies supervised fine-tuning on synthetic reasoning traces, and runs RL on chess puzzles.
   Discovers a joint pretraining-to-RL scaling law: post-RL reward scales predictably with both pretraining
   loss and RL compute budget, enabling principled allocation of compute across the two stages. Mechanistic
   analysis reveals that RL does not discover qualitatively new moves but reorganizes and concentrates
   existing competence — amplifying high-value moves and suppressing low-value ones that pretraining
   distributed probability mass across indiscriminately.

2. RL Post-Training Builds Compositional Reasoning Strategies [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Azwar Abdulsalam, Nishil Patel, Andrew Saxe
   Affiliation: UCL Gatsby Computational Neuroscience Unit
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.07646
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.07646
   Summary: Asks whether RL post-training merely amplifies primitive skills already latent in a pretrained
   model, or can compose them into genuinely new higher-level strategies. Studies this question in a fully
   observable rewrite-grammar environment where the pretraining distribution is known and controlled. A
   Transformer is pretrained on primitive symbol-rewrite chains and post-trained on composite Trace tasks
   with only binary final-answer reward. RL solves held-out composite problems that remain rarely solved
   by the pretrained model even under larger sampling budgets, while rejection fine-tuning improves early
   but plateaus. Trace analysis reveals that RL reorganizes primitive competence through a phased
   mechanism: first strengthening primitive reductions, then discovering valid sequential and parallel
   compositions of those primitives.

3. Video Generation Models are General-Purpose Vision Learners [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Letian Wang et al.
   Affiliation: Google DeepMind
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.09024
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09024
   Summary: Introduces GenCeption, which contends that large-scale text-to-video generation serves as
   a strong pre-training paradigm for computer vision, providing the spatiotemporal priors, vision-language
   alignment, and scalability required for general visual intelligence. GenCeption repurposes a pre-trained
   video generative diffusion backbone as a unified feed-forward perception model that performs diverse
   vision tasks steered by text instructions. Achieves state-of-the-art performance on depth, surface
   normal, and camera pose estimation, expression-referring segmentation, and 3D keypoint prediction.
   Demonstrates 7 to 500 times greater data efficiency than competing specialized models including
   DepthAnything3, SAM3, and VGGT-Omega. A model trained exclusively on synthetic human videos
   spontaneously generalizes to real-world footage and out-of-distribution objects such as animals.

4. Gemma 4 Technical Report [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Gemma Team (Sherif El Abd et al.)
   Affiliation: Google DeepMind
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.02770
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.02770
   Summary: Introduces Gemma 4, a new generation of open-weight, natively multimodal language models
   ranging from 2.3B to 31B parameters in dense and Mixture-of-Experts architectures. Proposes improved
   vision and audio encoders for all model sizes and a unified encoder-free architecture for the 12B model
   that ingests raw audio and image patches end-to-end. Integrates a thinking mode enabling models to
   generate explicit reasoning traces prior to answering. Improves inference speed, memory, and compute
   efficiency through targeted architectural choices. Establishes significant gains across STEM,
   multimodal, and long-context benchmarks, rivaling larger frontier open models in human-rated
   evaluations. Full weights, checkpoints, and inference code are released openly.

5. Less Experts, Faster Decoding: Cost-Aware Speculative Decoding for Mixture-of-Experts [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Jincheng Xie, Runheng Liu, Heyan Huang, Yawen Ling, Hanbin Dai, Yu Zheng, Wen Hu
   Affiliation: Beijing Institute of Technology
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.12696
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12696
   Summary: Identifies and addresses expert scattering, a previously uncharacterized failure mode of
   speculative decoding applied to Mixture-of-Experts models, in which confidence-driven draft selection
   routes high-probability tokens to disjoint expert sets, increasing expert-weight memory traffic and
   eliminating the memory-locality benefit that makes speculative decoding fast. Proposes EcoSpec, a
   cost-aware framework incorporating a lightweight expert predictor and a dynamic expert buffer that
   jointly optimizes draft acceptance likelihood and expert reuse. Evaluated on DeepSeek-V3.1 (671B),
   Qwen3-235B-A22B, and GPT-OSS-120B across reasoning, coding, question-answering, and dialogue
   benchmarks. EcoSpec consistently reduces active expert footprint and achieves up to 1.62x end-to-end
   decoding speedup over standard speculative decoding baselines.
