1. Nemotron-Labs-Diffusion: A Tri-Mode Language Model Unifying Autoregressive, Diffusion, and Self-Speculation Decoding [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Yonggan Fu, Lexington Whalen, Abhinav Garg, Chengyue Wu, Maksim Khadkevich, Nicolai Oswald, Enze Xie, Daniel Egert, Sharath Turuvekere Sreenivas, Shizhe Diao, Chenhan Yu, Ye Yu, Weijia Chen, Sajad Norouzi, Jingyu Liu, Shiyi Lan, Ligeng Zhu, Jin Wang, Jindong Jiang, Morteza Mardani, Mehran Maghoumi, Song Han, Ante Jukić, Nima Tajbakhsh, Jan Kautz, Pavlo Molchanov
   Affiliation: NVIDIA Research
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.05722
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05722
   Summary: Introduces a single language model that operates in three modes — autoregressive (AR),
   parallel diffusion, and self-speculation — trained jointly with a combined AR-diffusion objective.
   In self-speculation mode, the diffusion head drafts candidate tokens while the AR head verifies
   them, eliminating the need for a separate draft model and achieving 6.82 accepted tokens per step
   versus Eagle3's 2.75. On SPEED-Bench with SGLang on a GB200 GPU, Nemotron-Labs-Diffusion-8B
   delivers 4x higher throughput than Qwen3-8B at comparable accuracy.

2. Latent Programming Horizons in Coding Agents [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: André Silva, Han Tu, Martin Monperrus
   Affiliation: KTH Royal Institute of Technology, Stockholm, Sweden
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.05188
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.05188
   Summary: Probes the residual streams of language models acting as coding agents and finds that
   internal representations linearly encode properties of the evolving program — such as whether
   the code parses, passes its test suite, or introduces regressions — with AUC up to 0.83. More
   strikingly, these representations run ahead of the agent's own edits: probes can predict the
   outcome of future edits up to 25 steps before they are materialized. The authors term this the
   agent's "latent programming horizon," opening new avenues for mechanistic interpretability of
   software engineering agents.

3. Long-Horizon-Terminal-Bench: Testing the Limits of Agents on Long-Horizon Terminal Tasks with Dense Reward-Based Grading [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Zongxia Li, Zhongzhi Li, Yucheng Shi, Ruhan Wang, Junyao Yang, Zhichao Liu, Xiyang Wu, Anhao Li, Yue Yu, Ninghao Liu, Lichao Sun, Haotao Mi, Leowei Liang
   Affiliation: Tencent HY LLM Frontier; University of Maryland College Park; University of Georgia; University of Minnesota Twin Cities; Indiana University; Lehigh University; National University of Singapore; The Hong Kong Polytechnic University
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.08964
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.08964
   Summary: Presents a 46-task terminal benchmark spanning nine categories — experiment reproduction,
   software engineering, multimodal analysis, interactive games, and scientific computing — designed
   to test how well LLM agents sustain useful work in a containerized terminal over hundreds of steps.
   Unlike binary pass/fail benchmarks, LHTB decomposes each task into fine-grained subtasks with
   dense intermediate rewards and partial credit, enabling fairer comparison of agents on complex
   open-ended workflows. Grades are produced by hidden, rebuild-from-artifact verifiers to prevent
   self-reported progress gaming.

4. Neural Collapse Is Forbidden: Information Floors in Language Models [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Bruno Abrahao
   Affiliation: NYU Shanghai
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.09487
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09487
   Summary: Argues that within-class variance in language-model representations — commonly viewed
   as incomplete neural collapse — is actually obligatory information storage governed by a
   provable lower bound. Across 14 models spanning a 100x parameter range, macro-category structure
   accounts for only 4–12% of representational variance while within-token context carries 79–91%.
   The paper proves a converse information floor showing that within-category dispersion must be at
   least proportional to the conditional mutual information between tokens and context given the
   category, making full neural collapse information-theoretically impossible in next-token prediction.

5. Video Generation Models are General-Purpose Vision Learners [ECCV 2026]
   Authors: Letian Wang, Chuhan Zhang, Rishabh Kabra, Jasper Uijlings, Steven Waslander, Andrew Zisserman, Joao Carreira, Kaiming He, Misha Andriluka, Eduard Gabriel Bazavan, Andrei Zanfir, Cristian Sminchisescu
   Affiliation: Google DeepMind
   Venue/ID: ECCV 2026 | arXiv:2607.09024
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.09024
   Summary: Proposes GenCeption, which repurposes a pre-trained text-to-video diffusion backbone
   as a general-purpose, feed-forward perception model steered by text instructions. By analogy
   with how next-token prediction produced generalist NLP models, the authors argue that large-scale
   video generation pre-training provides the spatiotemporal priors, vision-language alignment, and
   scalability required for general visual intelligence. GenCeption achieves state-of-the-art
   performance on a diverse suite of tasks including depth and surface normal estimation, camera
   pose estimation, expression-referring segmentation, and 3D keypoint prediction.
