1. Ring-Zero: Scaling Zero RL to a Trillion Parameters for Emergent Reasoning [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Xinyu Tang, Qianggang Cao, Yurou Liu, Yuliang Zhan, Xiaochong Lan, Yifan Li, Yuchen Yan, Han Peng, Zican Dong, Zhenduo Zhang, et al.
   Affiliation: Ant Group InclusionAI
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.12395
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12395
   Summary: Scales zero reinforcement learning — RL with verifiable rewards and no human-annotated data — to
   one trillion parameters, the largest scale attempted under this paradigm. The authors introduce a stable
   training pipeline with clipped importance sampling, training-inference ratio correction, and mixed-precision
   control. Training reliably unfolds in two sequential phases: a discovery phase where the model expands
   reasoning boundaries by unlocking dormant pathways, then a sharpening phase that refines policy within
   those limits. At trillion-parameter scale, zero RL spontaneously produces five emergent behaviors —
   self-verification, context anxiety, anthropomorphism, structured formatting, and parallel reasoning —
   none of which were present at smaller scales. Ring-2.5-1T-Zero achieves 84.2% on AIME 2026 using
   only first-stage RL training across seven mathematical reasoning benchmarks.

2. Let RGB Be the Language of Vision [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Timing Yang, Jinrui Yang, Xinlong Li, Yuhan Wang, Haoran Li, Yanqing Liu, Guoyizhe Wei, Jixuan Ying, Chen Wei, Rama Chellappa, Yuyin Zhou, Cihang Xie, Alan Yuille, Feng Wang
   Affiliation: Johns Hopkins University, UC Santa Cruz, Carnegie Mellon University, Rice University
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.12450
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12450
   Summary: Introduces RINO (RGB In, RGB Out), a unified formulation that represents all visual information
   — depth maps, segmentation masks, surface normals, keypoints — as RGB images, and recasts all visual
   tasks as a common RGB-to-RGB image editing problem. By sharing the same encoder, decoder, and parameters
   across visual modalities, RINO enables a single backbone to transfer across perception tasks through a
   unified visual interface, by analogy with how language models operate over text. Built on a generic image
   editing backbone without task-specific fine-tuning, RINO demonstrates competitive zero-shot performance
   on dense understanding tasks including segmentation, depth estimation, surface normal prediction, and
   3D keypoint localization.

3. Knowledgeless Language Models: Suppressing Parametric Recall for Evidence-Grounded Language Modeling [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Roi Cohen, Yvan Carré, Nick Lechtenbörger, Hendrik Droste, Lucas Kerschke, Russa Biswas, Gerard de Melo, Jan Buys
   Affiliation: Hasso Plattner Institute, University of Cape Town
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.12831
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.12831
   Summary: Studies whether modifying the pretraining signal can systematically shift language models away
   from parametric recall and toward evidence-grounded reasoning. Introduces KLLMs (Knowledgeless Language
   Models), pretrained on corpora in which named entities are anonymized, removing the primary channel for
   entity-linked factual supervision. This intervention substantially reduces closed-book factual recall
   while consistently improving performance on tasks where relevant information is provided as context.
   Across multiple model scales, KLLMs outperform matched baselines on contextual question answering, fact
   verification, and hallucination detection benchmarks, offering a pretraining-level path toward more
   grounded, reliable language modeling.

4. TRACE: Turn-level Reward Assignment via Credit Estimation for Long-Horizon Agents [arXiv 2026]
   Authors: Leitian Tao, Baolin Peng, Wenlin Yao, Tao Ge, Hao Cheng, Mike Hang Wang, Jianfeng Gao, Sharon Li
   Affiliation: Microsoft Research, University of Wisconsin-Madison
   Venue/ID: arXiv:2607.13988
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.13988
   Summary: Addresses the credit assignment problem for multi-turn agents that solve complex tasks through
   extended sequences of tool interactions before producing a final answer. Outcome rewards become sparse
   and high-variance as trajectories grow to tens or hundreds of tool calls, and can be misleading because
   a failed rollout may contain many useful actions. TRACE represents rollouts as state transitions at
   tool-call boundaries, obtains gold-answer log-probabilities from a frozen reference model, transforms
   them into log-ratio state values, and derives per-action rewards as Temporal-Difference changes in those
   values. The resulting turn-level rewards provide dense, reliable training signal without requiring human
   annotation of intermediate steps.

5. Mask-Aware Policy Gradients for Diffusion Language Models [COLM 2026]
   Authors: Haran Raajesh, Kulin Shah, Adam Klivans, Philipp Krähenbühl
   Affiliation: University of Texas at Austin
   Venue/ID: COLM 2026 | arXiv:2607.15200
   URL: https://arxiv.org/abs/2607.15200
   Summary: Addresses reinforcement learning for Masked Diffusion Language Models (MDLMs), where existing
   approaches approximate the policy gradient by modeling only token predictions, ignoring the order in
   which positions are unmasked. The paper observes that MDLM generation involves two decisions at each
   step — what token to place and which positions to remask — and formalizes this as a two-stage action
   MDP where the policy gradient naturally decomposes into a token term and a masking term. The resulting
   mask-aware policy gradient is more principled than prior approximations and achieves state-of-the-art
   results on mathematical reasoning and coding benchmarks: 87.1% on GSM8K and 53.4% on MBPP.
