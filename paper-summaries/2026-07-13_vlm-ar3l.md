## Headline Finding

Designing reward functions for open-ended RL tasks is expensive and brittle. VLM-AR3L uses Vision-Language Models to provide two complementary reward signals — an absolute reward scoring individual states and a relative reward measuring progress between consecutive observations — without any environment-specific reward engineering. Evaluated on long-horizon Minecraft tasks from MineDojo, VLM-AR3L consistently outperforms prior VLM-based reward baselines across all four tested tasks.

---

## Key Findings (Inverted Pyramid)

### Level 1 — Main Result
VLM-AR3L decomposes the reward signal into two branches:
- **Absolute Reward Model (ARM):** A learned scorer that maps $(x_t, g)$ — current visual state and language goal — to a scalar evaluation $\hat{r}_t^{abs} \in [0,1]$
- **Relative Reward Model (RRM):** A pairwise comparator mapping $(x_t, x_{t+1}, g)$ to a progress signal $\hat{r}_t^{rel} \in [-1, 1]$

On MineDojo benchmarks:
- **Combat Spider:** +38% success rate over best VLM reward baseline
- **Milk Cow:** +29% success rate
- **Shear Sheep:** +41% success rate
- **Hunt Cow:** +33% success rate

### Level 2 — Core Mechanism
The absolute reward provides stability: even a noisy but consistent scalar goal-achievement estimate prevents reward collapse. The relative reward provides adaptivity: by comparing consecutive frames the model can detect local task progress or regression even when the absolute goal remains far away. The two signals are combined as a weighted sum $r_t = \alpha \hat{r}_t^{abs} + (1-\alpha) \hat{r}_t^{rel}$. The combination exploits the complementary failure modes of each branch — absolute rewards suffer from sparse signal late in long-horizon episodes; relative rewards suffer from local optima in state-comparison chains.

### Level 3 — Methodology
VLM preference labels are generated by prompting a VLM (LLaMA-Vision-3.1-8B) with:
1. For ARM: "On a scale of 0 to 1, how well does this frame show an agent achieving [goal]?" — generating scalar pseudo-labels for states sampled from policy rollouts
2. For RRM: "Does frame B show more progress toward [goal] than frame A?" — generating binary labels for consecutive-frame pairs

Both reward models are then trained as small neural networks (2-layer MLPs on top of frozen visual features) on the VLM-generated labels. The policy is trained using PPO with the combined reward signal.

### Level 4 — Technical Details
The total reward objective:
$$r_t = \alpha \cdot \sigma\!\left(f_{\phi}^{abs}(\text{enc}(x_t), \text{enc}(g))\right) + (1-\alpha) \cdot \tanh\!\left(f_{\psi}^{rel}(\text{enc}(x_t), \text{enc}(x_{t+1}), \text{enc}(g))\right)$$

where $\text{enc}(\cdot)$ is a frozen CLIP visual/text encoder, $f_\phi^{abs}$ is the absolute reward MLP, and $f_\psi^{rel}$ is the relative reward MLP. The VLM annotation cost is amortized over all training steps via offline label generation before RL begins.

The relative reward training objective maximizes agreement with VLM pairwise comparisons:
$$\mathcal{L}_{rel}(\psi) = -\mathbb{E}_{(x_t, x_{t+1}, y)}\!\left[y \log \sigma(f_\psi^{rel}) + (1-y) \log(1 - \sigma(f_\psi^{rel}))\right]$$

where $y \in \{0,1\}$ is the VLM's preference label.

## Why Existing Approaches Fall Short

Prior VLM-based reward methods (EUREKA, VPT-R, and similar) generate only absolute scalar rewards from VLM evaluations of individual frames. In long-horizon tasks, the absolute reward is near-zero for hundreds of steps before a sub-goal is reached, creating a sparse signal that RL algorithms struggle to propagate efficiently. Approaches that use only relative/preference-based rewards (RLHF-style) suffer from the opposite problem: pairwise comparisons cannot quantify absolute progress, leading to agents that cycle between states with locally better transitions rather than converging on the global goal. VLM-AR3L is the first method to explicitly combine both signal types under a unified training pipeline.

All results are task success rates averaged over 100 episodes. VLM-AR3L achieves 29–41% relative improvement over the strongest single-reward VLM baseline across tasks.

## Significance

This work demonstrates that the dichotomy between absolute reward models and preference-based RL need not be a design choice — both can be derived from a single VLM in a complementary fashion. The framework requires no human-annotated rewards, no environment-specific heuristics, and no oracle reward function, making it directly applicable to any visually-observable environment with language-expressible goals. As RL applications expand to more open-ended real-world domains — robotics, embodied agents, game-playing — the ability to automatically derive dense, well-shaped reward functions from VLMs at near-zero marginal cost could be transformative.
